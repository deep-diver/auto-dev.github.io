A useful development post usually answers four questions:

## 1. What problem were you solving?
Start with the actual constraint. "I built X" is less interesting than "deployment time doubled after we added Y".

## 2. What options did you consider?
Even a short list helps readers understand the trade-offs and not just the final choice.

## 3. What changed?
Show the implementation, the architecture, the commands, or the diagrams that mattered.

## 4. What did you learn?
The interesting part is often not the fix but the lesson that made the fix obvious in hindsight.

If a post includes those four sections, it is usually already better than most internal status updates pretending to be engineering writing.
